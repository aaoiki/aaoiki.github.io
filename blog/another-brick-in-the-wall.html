Another Brick in the Wall? Or Why I Left Work to Finish My Degree Education

January 1, 2019

This post is exactly three years old. I have it in my drafts since the beginning of 2016. The first draft has nothing to do with its current form. My opinions have greatly changed since then (it’s a good way to self-reflection, I must say). The original title was “Another Brick in the wall?”. I added the second or-part to it to reflect what it is about right now.

Originally, this post was going to be a long rant about CS education, however; as I said, my opinions have changed since that time. This post is now about why CS education is important for future, wanna-be software developers.

By the end of 2017, I left my work at Robusta Studio to go back to school and finish my CS degree after stalling for two years. I’m sure the folks at /r/cscareerquestions will probably have a lot of advice regarding this matter, on why it’s useless to leave a full-time paying job and go back to school. Well, I beg to differ. Having a CS degree specially for young unexperienced developers is as important as acquiring programming skills. Here’s why you may need to consider going for a CS degree.

Theoretical Fundamentals

This is the base of computer science education; the theory behind computation. You cannot take on computer science without learning tons of theoretical materials. This theoretical knowledge prepares you to be a better programmer by understanding why and how a certain technology works behind the scene. It’s true that you probably won’t need most of the theoretical stuff in your day-to-day job but…

Time Management

With literally tens of projects each year, you need to learn to manage your time properly between putting the effort to study and doing the school projects. This is something I learned the hard way. Time management is an invaluable skill in software development career. You need to learn how to estimate your projects and give reasonable timeline for each task.

Networking

If you doubt my other reasons for going into computer science, you cannot deny this one. Having a network of like-minded peers and friends is the first thing that comes to my mind when I think about pros of computer science education. This network will probably help you later in your career when you find out that most company hiring is unannounced to the public but rather, companies depend on their own network of developers to hire from.

Credibility

Again, another reason for your software development career. Although there may be a general direction of not requiring a degree, many companies still do require a CS degree from their candidates. And although there are companies that may not require a CS degree to hire you, having a CS degree makes you look more credible to your future employer specially if it’s your first job and you’re without experience.

Social Considerations

Society is not ready to accept that education does not have to come from universities.

A brief story

He gave me a version of Visual Basic 5 on a weird sticky thing called a flash drive (yup, they were new). I installed VB and started looking up the internet for VB scripts and how to run them.
